The psychology of a disorganized person involves a combination of behavioral, cognitive, and emotional factors, which can stem from a lack of skills, certain mental health conditions like ADHD or depression, or a history of trauma. Common signs include trouble with focus, time management, and prioritizing tasks, which often leads to frustration, anxiety, and feelings of overwhelm. While disorganization can range from everyday messiness to severe behavioral inconsistencies, it can also be associated with a disorganized attachment style and may, in its most extreme forms (often seen in conditions like schizophrenia), involve erratic and unusual behaviors.  
Behavioral and cognitive factors
Trouble with executive functions: A disorganized person may struggle with planning, time management, and prioritizing tasks, leading to procrastination and difficulty completing projects. 
Lack of focus: They can be easily distracted by their environment, feelings, or thoughts, making it hard to concentrate and leading to reduced efficiency and decision-making ability. 
Cognitive overload: Clutter and disorganization can have a cumulative effect on the brain, draining cognitive resources and reducing the ability to focus and remember things. 
Inconsistent behavior: In some cases, disorganization can manifest as self-contradictory or inconsistent actions, such as dressing inappropriately for the weather, which is sometimes seen in conditions like schizophrenia. 
Emotional factors
Negative emotions: Disorganization is often accompanied by significant negative emotions like frustration, anxiety, guilt, shame, and overwhelm. 
Negative self-view: A disorganized attachment style, often linked to a history of inconsistent or frightening caregiving, is associated with a negative view of both oneself and others. 
Potential causes
Mental health conditions: Disorganization can be a symptom of various conditions, including ADHD, depression, and, in more severe cases, schizophrenia. 
Lack of skills: Sometimes disorganization is due to a simple lack of learned skills rather than a deeper psychological issue. 
Trauma and attachment: A history of trauma, unresolved loss, or abuse can contribute to disorganization, particularly a disorganized attachment style. 
Perfectionism: Interestingly, perfectionism can also be a cause of disorganization, as it can lead to indecision and a fear of starting tasks.
